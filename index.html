01

Why Does Australia Use Kilojoules Instead of Calories?

Australia follows the International System of Units (SI), which uses the joule as the standard unit of energy. Since food energy is measured in thousands of joules, the kilojoule (kJ) is the official unit used on all Australian and New Zealand food labels, as required by Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ).

In contrast, the United States, United Kingdom, and many other countries use kilocalories (kcal) — commonly just called "calories" — on their food packaging. So when an American recipe says a meal is "500 calories," an Australian label for the same meal would read approximately 2,092 kJ.

Neither unit is better or more accurate — they simply measure the same thing in different scales.

02

The Simple Conversion Formula

Converting kJ to calories is straightforward. There are two versions depending on which type of "calorie" you mean:

1. Kilojoules to Kilocalories (kcal) — the most common

Calories (kcal) = Kilojoules ÷ 4.184
Or for a quick mental estimate: Calories ≈ Kilojoules ÷ 4.2

2. Kilojoules to small calories (cal) — rarely used in nutrition

cal = Kilojoules ÷ 0.004184

For everyday diet and nutrition purposes, always use the ÷ 4.2 formula. When people say "calories" in the context of food and dieting, they almost always mean kilocalories (kcal). You can also use the free converter at kjtocalories.com to do this instantly without any math.

How to Read an Australian Food Label

Australian nutrition panels are regulated by FSANZ and must follow a standard format. Here's what to look for:

1
Find the Energy Row

The nutrition panel always lists Energy in kJ (and sometimes kcal alongside it). It shows two columns: Per Serving and Per 100g.

2
Check the Serving Size

This is where many people get caught out. A label might say "serving size: 30g" with 540 kJ per serving — but if you eat 60g, you're consuming 1,080 kJ (~257 calories).

3
Convert Using the Formula

Divide the kJ value by 4.2. Or head to kjtocalories.com for an instant result.

4
Use Per 100g for Comparisons

When comparing two brands of yoghurt, always use the per 100g column — serving sizes can differ between brands.

Example: A muesli bar shows 820 kJ per serve
820 ÷ 4.2 = ~195 calories
